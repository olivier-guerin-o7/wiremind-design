Market view
Redesign

How to improve this widget? This prototype is my answer. Incomplete by design, but grounded in a short analysis and built with analysts in mind: how they navigate dense data under time pressure.

These notes are a mix of recommendations and solutions, either partially or fully implemented in this prototype.

See original
Analyst profile Expert user, not a learner. Works across multiple departures per session under real time pressure — not millisecond speed, but an hours-to-days horizon where every decision is a bet on future demand. Operational decisions are immediately live: visible to customers within minutes, with no default undo. The interface must support someone who already knows the tool deeply, operating under genuine uncertainty about demand, competitor intent, and model reliability. Critically: session length compounds fatigue — a design that works for decision 1 must still work at decision 20.
Accuracy Misreading a signal produces a wrong bet with direct revenue consequences. If the analyst can't trust what they're seeing, they either freeze or act on a bad read — both costly. Every signal must represent reality correctly, and the interface must make the right read the easiest read.
Error prevention In interfaces where actions have immediate, hard-to-reverse real-world consequences, error prevention is structural — not a matter of user carefulness. The interface must make the wrong action harder to reach than the right one, add friction before irreversible commits, and support recovery where possible. The cost of a mistake scales with context: the less reversible the action, the more protection the design must provide.
Cognitive load & fatigue Two distinct dimensions: per-decision (working memory cost in the moment) and cumulative/session (fatigue across many departures). Defaults matter more than options: the analyst should not have to reconfigure the view per market. Computed indicators reduce per-decision load — but too many add a new scanning burden. Balance is required.
Relevance Information not needed for this decision is noise. Noise forces filtering — and filtering is cognitive work. Every element must earn its place in the default view. Removal is often the highest-impact design move.
Anti-over-automation & exception visibility Over-automation disengages the analyst — a passive analyst stops noticing exceptions. Any rule that can be applied 100% consistently should be computed. But raw signal must remain visible alongside indicators: the model can't catch what it didn't compute, and the analyst needs peripheral vision for the unusual case.
Trust calibration Signals should communicate how much to trust them. A forecast with no confidence range is more dangerous than no forecast — the analyst may treat a rough estimate as a reliable target. Not covered in this prototype.
Non-expert perspective This redesign was approached without deep domain expertise in revenue management. I invested enough time in the widget to form meaningful design hypotheses — but almost certainly missed important context or misread some mechanics. Each proposal should be validated against real operational knowledge before acting on it.
Sizes are off This prototype runs at a magnified scale — larger than production dimensions. Small content (figures, labels, dense data) would likely break visually when reduced to real size. This was a deliberate tradeoff: working at this scale let us test and iterate on design decisions faster within a limited timeframe. A full rework at real dimensions would be needed to validate these proposals in context.
Charts and radar: macro pass only I deliberately scoped out deep reworks of the loading curves and radar zones. Given the time available, I prioritised macro layout and interaction decisions over fine-grained data visualisation refinements — which would require closer domain and data expertise to do well.

Color system audit AU Grid and Competition both use red — one for price tier, one for threat level. We identified the conflict but scoped out a full remap. The fix would require a shared color token system across both panels, separating encoding by hue family or shape.
recommendationCognitive loadRelevance
Information density vs. cognitive load — find the right balance Every indicator added to the widget is a bet: that it will be used often enough to justify the scanning cost it imposes on every session, including the ones where it's irrelevant. The tension is real — analysts need enough signal to act with confidence, but too many signals force filtering, and filtering is cognitive work. Design can help stretch that balance: strong visual hierarchy, disciplined use of color, and progressive disclosure can fit more information into the same space while keeping it readable. This prototype explores that direction. But design alone cannot resolve it — only user testing with real analysts across real sessions can tell Wiremind where the threshold actually sits. Strong design choices made without validation remain hypotheses. They should be treated as such until tested.

Should some Loading Curves and Competition indicators move into the AU Grid? The AU Grid currently shows ground truth and controls only. But both Loading Curves and Competition produce signals the analyst uses to decide per bucket. If those were computed and surfaced directly in each AU Grid row — a threat badge ("2 red competitors at −£15"), a gap vs. Forecast target per bucket — the analyst could make a per-row decision without cross-referencing the other panels. If that enrichment happens, the AU Grid earns more horizontal space. Without it, the resize has no justification.

Persistent context layer The original top bar held only a flight reference — no data before opening a panel. Six KPIs now form the macro bar in a deliberate left-to-right order that mirrors how an analyst naturally assesses a flight: Dep. in (how much time?), Load % (how full?), Max Leg (what's the capacity ceiling?), Open bucket (what am I selling and at what price?), Fcst / Optim gap (what is the model recommending?), Demand index (what is the market doing?). The analyst arrives at any panel already oriented — no hunting, no context switch.

Calm strip The six KPIs share a single neutral typographic register — no status colors, no icons competing for attention. Color is reserved for genuine signals only: the departure countdown turns colored as departure approaches, and the alerts badge activates when there is something to act on. Everything else stays neutral by design, so when color appears, it carries weight.

Alerts The alert bell sits deliberately outside the KPI strip, at the right edge. The badge tells the analyst in one glance whether anything is urgent. Clicking it opens a compact popover anchored to the trigger — a quick scan without leaving the current panel. Its separation from the routine check is intentional: it is reactive, not part of the left-to-right read. When the badge is empty, the analyst never needs to look at it.

Inverted contrast — test The macro bar carries always-present context — it never changes, never requires action. Giving it a dark background visually separates it from the active panels below. The eye can't simultaneously process two different contrast registers, which may help focus attention on the decision area. Try the three variants and compare.

Visual de-emphasis — structure unchanged The toolbar content is kept as-is. The only change is visual: a grey background distinguishes it from the macro bar above and the panels below — the same logic as zebra rows in a dense table, where alternating registers reduce visual fatigue and lower error risk. Within the toolbar, two values still stand out: £277 (current open bucket price) and £204 (LLM reference fare). Together they frame the core pricing question at a glance — where am I vs. where the model thinks I should be. They are less prominent than in the original, but still the first things the analyst's eye finds in the toolbar.

Scannability & error prevention Three targeted changes:
  • Header background removed — white header reduces noise for experts; still readable for newcomers.
  • Selected row: white bg + top/bottom border — lifts the active row without washing over values. Price and B columns darken to close the visual loop. Error prevention.
  • Zebra striping removed — too few columns to justify it. Row hover (light blue tint on data columns) handles scannability instead, keeping the resting state clean.
doneError prevention
Lightweight friction for bucket commits A and Au are the only editable columns — direct commits to the live system, visible to customers within minutes, with no default undo. Changing a value triggers a brief undo toast, giving the analyst a short window to catch a mis-entry before it lands. The toast duration should be user-configurable in settings: an experienced analyst may want a shorter window to stay in flow, while a less confident one may need more time.

The analyst gets the competitive read inline, without cross-referencing the Competition panel — one decision, one place to look.

Fewer lines, clearer signal The original had 7+ overlays competing visually — a line for every model layer, all visible by default. We reduced to 2 lines per mode: Current + Reference in History mode (what happened), Forecast + Optim in Forecast mode (where it's going). The most important signals are now the only ones on screen. Other overlays remain accessible on demand via the icon button top-right of the strip — not implemented in this prototype, but the affordance is present.

Gap made explicit — numbers above the chart The Forecast↔Optim gap is the decision. Rather than making the analyst read it off two lines on a graph, the KPI strip above surfaces it directly: Current PAX / Forecast D-0 / Optim D-0 / Gap −8. The left-to-right order is deliberate — it mirrors the analyst's mental model: where am I now, where am I projected to end up, where could I go if I follow the algorithm, and what is the gap between those two? Each value earns its position. The number is impossible to miss. The chart stays for pattern reading and price elasticity; the strip handles the fast scan.

Split into two explicit modes: History vs. Forecast History mode: Current + Reference (what happened). Forecast mode: Forecast + Optim (where it's going). Separates past fact from future projection — reduces visual competition and cognitive load per read. The deeper intent is a zoom effect: by stripping away the historical curve, Forecast mode focuses the entire graph on the D-0 projection window — the part where the analyst's decision actually lives. The signal density stays the same; the noise floor drops. The toggle (bottom left) keeps the switch effortless and the full picture always one click away.
doneCognitive loadRelevance
Overlay controls moved to the bottom right In the original, overlay toggles sat at the top right of the panel — visible before the analyst had even looked at the chart, creating a decision about configuration before a decision about data. Moving them to the bottom right reverses that order: the analyst reads the chart first, then reaches for more if needed. Position earns its meaning here — bottom signals secondary, right signals optional. The controls are still one click away (Bookings, Daily pick-up, Live sales, Pax type, and a "more" overflow), but they no longer compete with the chart for first attention. This is the "depth on demand" principle made spatial.

Threat ticker + expandable fares table The ticker strip at the top of the Competition panel surfaces computed threat counts at a glance — 3 threat, 8 watch, 8 ok, 2 safe — without requiring the analyst to count planes on the radar. Each level is color-coded and always visible. For the analyst who needs precision (exact prices, flight numbers, availability), clicking the ticker opens a structured table of all 21 competitor flights, sorted by threat level. Each row shows carrier, flight, time offset, seats available, 7-day price trend, route type (direct vs. stop), price, and delta vs. the current open bucket. The radar stays for spatial context; the ticker handles the fast read; the table handles the deep dive. Three layers, one interaction.

Radar readability — zones removed, lines in The original radar used colored background zones (red/orange/green bands) to encode threat level spatially. These were visually heavy and introduced a second color system that conflicted with the price sphere colors in the AU Grid. In this redesign, zones are replaced with horizontal dotted line separators per level and vertical dashed dividers for time proximity — lighter, less intrusive, and sufficient for orientation once the analyst knows the layout. This is a deliberate bet: that experienced analysts navigating the radar daily will orient by the lines without needing the filled zones. It may not hold for new users or low-frequency analysts — only user testing can confirm whether the lines are readable enough on their own.
doneAccuracy
Airline labels — an accessible alternative The radar uses plane icons colored by carrier. For analysts who are color-blind or in contexts where color differentiation is unreliable, the eye button (top right of the radar) switches icons to 2-letter IATA carrier codes — FR, VY, U2, IB. Identification by shape rather than hue: fully legible without any color perception. A small toggle, a meaningful inclusion.
